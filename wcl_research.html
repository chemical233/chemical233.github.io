    • Research

            Nowadays, accompanied by the electric vehicle widespread, the conventional lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) are difficult to meet the market demands of large endurance and high security. Deriving from the inflammability of inorganic solid electrolyte, all-solid-state lithium batteries (ASSLBs) are able to achieve high safety which are ideal substitutes for regular LIBs. Furthermore, on account of the well compatibility between solid electrolyte and lithium metal, lithium metal is capable of serving as anode active substance. Benefitting from that, the energy density of ASSLBs are much higher than common LIBs.
            Our group focus on the research of material science, interface engineering, reaction mechanism, and industrialization of ASSLBs. We have synthesized solid electrolyte materials through advanced technology and realized ASSLBS with excellent performance. (Sci. adv., 2021, 7(37): eabh1896; Adv. Mater., 2023, 35(5): 2207234; Adv. Energy Mater., 2021, 11(24): 2100210) Meanwhile, advanced characterization tools are also carried out to correlate microscopic phenomena with macroscopic performance in our works. (Joule, 2022, 6(8): 1770-1781; Angew. Chem. Int. Ed., 2023: e202300962; Nano Energy, 2020, 76: 105015) Specifically, the research interests of our group comprise the following themes:

    1. Solid-State Electrolyte Design

            Solid-state electrolyte is the critical portion in ASSLIBs which makes great impact on the performance of ASSLBs. We are absorbed in designing practical solid-state electrolyte by means of developing synthetic techniques of solid-state electrolyte bulk materials and preparation technologies of solid-state membrane. Oxide-based, sulfide-based, and halide-based solid-state electrolytes are all in our research interests. For instance, we have reported ammonium-assisted wet chemistry to synthesize various solid-state halide electrolytes. Such halide electrolytes, including Li3YCl6, Li3ScCl6, Li3YBr6, and Li3ErCl6, are obtained with high ionic conductivity of 0.345, 1.25, 1.09, and 0.407 mS cm-1, respectively.     2. Interface Design

             Large interface resistance originating from poor solid-solid contact and detrimental interfacial reactions is one of the main challenges for the development of ASSLBs. We are concerned about the interface modification strategies to optimize the solid-solid contact and suppress the interfacial reactions, including surface coating, surface modification, and building buffer layer. In our previous works, we have synthesized lithium-rich oxide with a coating layer of Li3PO4 by atom layer deposition. Profit from the coating layer, the interfacial reactions between lithium-rich oxide and electrolyte are restrained and the transport of Li+ are promoted at the meantime. This work is the first demonstrated LLO-based ASSLBs using a solid-state halide electrolyte. Furthermore, we also have utilized the strategy of constructing buffer layer to eliminating the interfacial reactions like the solid-state sandwich electrolyte we applied in Li-Cu battery and the LixSiOy layer we have made at the garnet electrolyte surface.

    3. Advanced Electrode Materials

             This research focus on exploring new electrode materials and developing advanced modification techniques to reduce the incompatibility between electrode materials and solid-state electrolytes. In regard to cathode materials, Li-rich and Ni-rich cathodes are what we are most interested in. As for anode materials, we pay close attention to lithium metal and silicon-based materials. We have prepared single-crystal Li(Ni0.5Mn0.3 Co0.2)O2 and applied it as the cathode material for ASSLBs for the first time. Apart from that, recently, we reported one-step calcination to synthesize bulk-to-surface modified LiNi0.9Co0.09Mo0.01O2 (NCMo90) featuring a 5 nm Li2TiO3 coating on the surface, a Mo-rich Li+/Ni2+ superlattice at the sub-surface, and Ti-doping in the bulk.

    4. All-solid-state battery engineering

             This research aims to find the solutions for the systematic problems of ASSLBs and establish a complete R&D chain from basic research of battery materials to battery industrialization. The specific research contents include the techniques of preparing electrode and solid electrolyte membrane, the assembly technology of ASSLBs, and the feasible and cost-effective means for mass production. Both the solvent-free approach to fabricate ultrathin and freestanding solid electrolyte membranes and the ammonium-assisted wet chemistry to synthesize halide electrolyte we have developed are concentrate on solving the challenge for mass production of solid-state electrolyte.

    5. Advanced in-situ characterization

             Recently, various in-situ characterization techniques have developed to probe the interfacial chemical and structural information of ASSLBs. Our group have utilized in-situ Raman spectroscopy and X-ray diffraction to attain better understanding about the interfacial chemical and structural evolution of ASSLBs. In the next works, the application of advanced in-situ characterization techniques in the ASSLBs will get our focus.
